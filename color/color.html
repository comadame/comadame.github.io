Color

Additive

is color created by mixing a number of different light colors, with red, green, and blue being the primary colors normally used in additive color system.

Subtractive

a subtractive color model explains the mixing of a limited set of dyes, inks, paint pigments or natural colorants to create a wider range of colors, each the result of partially or completely subtracting (that is, absorbing) some wavelengths of light and not others.

Theory of Color

Primary

a group of colors from which all other colors can be obtained by mixing.

Secondary

a color resulting from the mixing of two primary colors.

Tertiary

the resulting color formed when an equal amount of a primary and a secondary color are mixed.

Color Models

Analogous

are groups of three colors that are next to each other on the color wheel, with one being the dominant color, which tends to be a primary or secondary color, and a tertiary. Red, orange, and red-violet are examples.

Monochromatic

are groups of three colors that are next to each other on the color wheel, with one being the dominant color, which tends to be a primary or secondary color, and a tertiary. Red, orange, and red-violet are examples.

Triadic

A triadic color scheme uses colors that are evenly spaced around the color wheel. Triadic color harmonies tend to be quite vibrant, even if you use pale or unsaturated versions of your hues.

Complementary

colors directly opposite each other in the color spectrum, such as red and green or blue and orange, that when combined in the right proportions, produce white light.
